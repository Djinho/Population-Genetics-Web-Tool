ADMIXTURE ANALYSIS


Admixture analysis is used to infer the ancestry of individuals by determining the proportion of an individual's ancestry that comes from each of several potential source populations. This can provide insights into the historical migrations and mixtures of populations.


This analysis can be crucial for understanding the genetic structure of populations and for studying the genetic basis of diseases. By identifying the ancestral proportions in an individual's genome, researchers can better account for genetic variation in association studies.


Admixture analysis is used to infer the ancestry of individuals by determining the proportion of an individual's ancestry that comes from each of several potential source populations. This can provide insights into the historical migrations and mixtures of populations. This analysis can be crucial for understanding the genetic structure of populations and for studying the genetic basis of diseases. By identifying the ancestral proportions in an individual's genome, researchers can better account for genetic variation in association studies.


ADMIXTURE is caused by interbreeding between isolated populations and this results in a mixture of alleles from different ancestral populations. Analysis of this allows classification of individuals with unknown ancestry into discrete populations [1]. ‘Ancestral’ or ‘parental’ refers to the previously isolated populations, whilst ‘admixed’ refers to the new population [2].


Several software tools, such as STRUCTURE, and ADMIXTURE facilitate these analyses by employing sophisticated algorithms designed to handle large genomic datasets. The outcomes of admixture analysis are pivotal for understanding genetic diversity, mapping genetic diseases, and providing ancestry information in personal genetic testing. Bayesian principle of likelihood comparison is used for STRUCTURE and prior information can be used to support further shaping of analysis. A similar method is used for ADMIXTURE but the population parameter calculation is a lot quicker and therefore allows for use of much larger marker sets. However, linkage disequilibrium is not taken into account for ADMIXTURE [3].
