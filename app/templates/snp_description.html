SNP ANALYSIS


Humans mostly have identical genomes therefore only a small proportion of genetic differences have caused the immense variation seen in individuals. All of these changes are due to genetic differences between individuals therefore identifying and observing the single base changes, also known as single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), is ideal [1].


The fixation index (FST) is used to measure how these populations differ based on their genetics. For our FST values, we used the general formula made by Sewell Wright [2]. This formula is described as: FST = s^2/¯p(1-¯p), where s^2 is the sample variance of allele frequencies and ¯p is the mean [3]. We calculated weighted averages using our sample size without incorporating any covariates, focusing solely on allele frequencies. We then computed the average allele frequency across all single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and applied a single FST calculation for the entire dataset.


For more details about results and how we obtained them, please refer to our documentation.
